\blocktitle{2.97}{9.42}{17.11}{Introduction / Abstract}
\textblock{3.45}{11.05}{16.15}{\smallbody\RaggedRight
Sports broadcasts contain high-value court logos and wall banners that can become dynamic, audience-specific advertising inventory. The hard part is realism: a replacement sponsor must follow broadcast perspective, preserve shadows and lighting, and remain behind players during occlusion.

We built a software-only pipeline for tennis. It removes the MELBOURNE court text, reconstructs the court surface, and composites a new sponsor while preserving player foreground masks.

The system is designed as an offline research prototype today, but every component maps to a future live-broadcast stack: segmentation, tracking, inpainting, color correction, and compositing.
}

\blocktitle{2.97}{17.65}{17.11}{Objectives}
\textblock{3.45}{19.05}{16.15}{\smallbody
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.0em,itemsep=0.10em]
  \item Replace painted court logos with arbitrary sponsor graphics.
  \item Preserve player occlusion, shoe motion blur, shadows, and court texture.
  \item Avoid proprietary venue hardware and sport-specific supervised training.
  \item Measure realism with color, temporal, and geometric stability metrics.
\end{itemize}
}

\textblock{3.45}{23.35}{16.15}{\tinybody\RaggedRight
We focus on a hard clip where the player walks across the logo. This forces the system to handle both surface replacement and foreground preservation in the same frames.
}

\blocktitle{22.27}{9.42}{17.11}{Materials / Methods}
\textblock{22.75}{11.05}{16.15}{\smallbody\RaggedRight
\textbf{Pass 1: segmentation.} Mask R-CNN initializes the player; SAM2.1 propagates masks through the clip. The inpaint region is the court logo area minus a dilated player mask, so the model does not erase legs.

\textbf{Pass 2: compositing.} DiffuEraser removes the painted text. We restore luminance, suppress residue, warp the new sponsor with homography, and blend with MatAnyone 2 soft alpha.

This ordering matters: first remove the existing logo from the court, then add the new sponsor. Directly pasting a new logo leaves ghost text, hard rectangular borders, and foreground-player artifacts.
}

\imageblock{23.05}{18.25}{15.55}{figures/midterm/sam-architecture.png}

\blocktitle{22.27}{22.85}{17.11}{Pipeline}
\textblock{22.75}{24.30}{16.15}{\smallbody
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.15em,itemsep=0.10em]
  \item Detect the player and propagate a temporally consistent SAM2.1 mask.
  \item Build a player-aware court-logo mask for safe inpainting.
  \item Remove original text, then restore court color and illumination.
  \item Fit/track the sponsor quadrilateral and composite with soft matting.
\end{enumerate}
}

\blocktitle{22.27}{30.50}{17.11}{Why Inpaint First?}
\textblock{22.75}{31.95}{16.15}{\tinybody\RaggedRight
Direct overlay keeps old text visible underneath the new logo. Inpainting first creates a clean court surface, then compositing controls the sponsor placement, opacity, and player edges.
}

\blocktitle{41.57}{21.55}{17.11}{Key Challenges}
\textblock{42.05}{23.00}{16.15}{\smallbody
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.0em,itemsep=0.10em]
  \item \textbf{Jitter:} small corner errors become visible shaking in broadcast overlays.
  \item \textbf{Occlusion:} players must stay in front of the sponsor, not be erased or painted over.
  \item \textbf{Speed:} the long-term target is live processing near 30 fps.
\end{itemize}
}

\blocktitle{41.57}{29.15}{17.11}{Conclusions}
\textblock{42.05}{30.60}{16.15}{\smallbody
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.15em,itemsep=0.10em]
  \item Static masks fail: video inpainting reconstructs missing pixels from temporal context and can bring the original text back.
  \item Player-aware masks are the correct architecture for court-floor ad replacement.
  \item DiffuEraser removes text more aggressively than ProPainter, but requires cleanup for player smears.
  \item MatAnyone 2 improves fast-motion player boundaries over binary masks.
\end{enumerate}
}
